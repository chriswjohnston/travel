Trip 06 · June 2040 · Age 14 · Grade 8 — Elementary School Graduation

Newfoundland

Theme: Atlantic Systems Graduation Trip Age 14

Mark JJ's elementary school graduation with the wild Atlantic at its most raw and elemental. Iceberg Alley. Humpback whales. 60,000 nesting gannets. Puffins at arm's length. And the story of a fishery — the Grand Banks cod — that fed the world for 500 years and collapsed in 15.

Iceberg Alley · Newfoundland & Labrador
Humpback Whale — Witless Bay
Atlantic Puffin — Elliston
Iceberg — Twillingate
Northern Gannets — Cape St. Mary's
Purpose

Mark JJ's elementary graduation with a genuinely wild, emotionally significant trip. Iceberg Alley, humpback and minke whales, and puffin colonies accessible by small boat. This is not a resort trip — it's a raw Atlantic experience that builds ecological literacy, connects climate systems, and marks the transition from elementary to high school science.

Farm Connection + Life Stage

At 14, JJ is entering high school with a strong ecological foundation. Newfoundland shows him the fishing community side of ocean life — how Atlantic ecosystems shaped human culture, and how the cod collapse of 1992 changed an entire province. The working harbour towns connect ecology to economics to culture. This is the trip where marine biology stops being a hobby and starts feeling like a calling.

Itinerary

8-Day Plan

Day
1
Fly to St. John's + Signal Hill
St. John's, NL
Fly into St. John's. Drive up Signal Hill for the first view of the North Atlantic — cold, grey, immense. This ocean has a completely different character from the Caribbean. Visit the Battery neighbourhood clinging to the cliffs above the Narrows. Fish and chips at a harbour-side restaurant. Check IcebergFinder.com to plan optimal iceberg viewing days.
Signal Hill viewpointThe Narrows harbourBattery neighbourhood walkIceberg tracking app check
Learning focus: the Atlantic feels different because it is different — colder, darker, and more productive than the Caribbean.
Day
2
The Rooms + Cod History + Drive North
St. John's → Twillingate
Morning: The Rooms provincial museum — excellent natural history, ocean science, and Beothuk exhibits. The cod fishery timeline is a powerful exhibit connecting ecology to economics. Drive northwest toward Twillingate (5 hours). Stop at Terra Nova National Park coastal lookout. Arrive in Twillingate, walk the harbour, talk to local fishers about the iceberg season.
The Rooms museumCod collapse exhibitTerra Nova coastal stopTwillingate harbour + local fishers
Learning focus: when the cod collapsed in 1992, 30,000 people lost their jobs in one season. Communities adapted — some toward crab and shrimp, some toward icebergs and tourism.
Day
3
Iceberg Boat Tour + Humpback Whales
Twillingate offshore
The core experience. Zodiac tour with an experienced operator — within 50m of a berg. You can hear it creak and crack, feel the cold air radiating off it, and see the turquoise subsurface ice. These bergs calved from Greenland glaciers 10,000–15,000 years ago and have been drifting 2–3 years to reach Newfoundland. Humpback whales often feed in the same cold water around iceberg meltwater.
Zodiac iceberg tourIceberg proximity and soundHumpback whale feeding observationSeabird flock ID
Learning focus: the Labrador Current carried this ice 3,000km from Greenland. Ocean currents are conveyor belts for nutrients, temperature, organisms, and ice.
Morning tours have calmer water and better light. Dress for cold — even in June, air off the ice is significant.
Day
4
Drive to Witless Bay — Puffins + Whales
Witless Bay
Drive south to Witless Bay, home to the largest Atlantic puffin colony in North America — over 260,000 pairs. Boat tour through the islands gives close views of puffins in flight, on the water, and ferrying sand eels to burrows. Humpback whales are common feeders here in June — the combination of puffins diving from above and humpbacks lunging from below, both targeting the same ball of capelin, is extraordinary.
Witless Bay puffin boat tourAtlantic puffin colonyHumpback lunge-feedingMurres and shearwater ID
Learning focus: puffins spend 90% of their lives at sea. Their bill design allows them to carry 10–12 sand eels at once — a remarkable engineering solution.
Day
5
Cape St. Mary's Ecological Reserve
Cape St. Mary's
Cape St. Mary's is the most accessible seabird spectacle in North America. A 1.5km coastal walk leads to Bird Rock — a sea stack home to 60,000 nesting northern gannets at arm's length. The noise and smell are overwhelming. Also: murres, razorbills, kittiwakes. The coastal trail shows extraordinary geology — 500-million-year-old Cambrian rock.
Bird Rock gannet colonyCape St. Mary's coastal trailCambrian rock geologyFog and Atlantic weather
Learning focus: the gannet colony recovered after decades of protection. Recovery is possible — when humans get out of the way, ecosystems respond.
Day
6
Bonavista — Puffins at Ground Level
Bonavista Peninsula
Elliston near Bonavista: puffins nest in burrows right at ground level on the open headland. Sit 2–3m from them while they go about their business — no boat required. This is the most intimate seabird experience possible. Cape Bonavista lighthouse where John Cabot made North American landfall in 1497 — launching the Grand Banks cod fishery that eventually collapsed 500 years later.
Elliston puffin headlandGround-level puffin observationCape Bonavista lighthouseTrinity outport village walk
Learning focus: the same coastline that supported the most productive fishery in human history for 500 years was stripped bare in 15 years of industrial trawling. The puffins are still here. The cod are not.
Day
7
Graduation Day — JJ's Choice + Letter Writing
JJ's choice
JJ's graduation day. Let him choose the activity: repeat the iceberg tour, return to the puffins, coastal hike, or The Rooms museum. Evening: graduation dinner in St. John's. Celebrate the transition from elementary to high school. Then: write the graduation letter — sealed, to be opened in Alaska at age 18. Contents: favourite experience so far, one question you can't yet answer, what you hope the Alaska trip will be.
JJ's choice activityGraduation dinnerWrite and seal the graduation letter
Learning focus: the arc from Disney at age 3 to Newfoundland at age 14 is visible now. Write a letter to your 18-year-old self about what you've seen and what you want to know.
This letter ritual is one of the most meaningful parts of the whole plan — take time with it and seal it properly.

Practical Logistics
  • Check IcebergFinder.com and CIS iceberg tracking before and during the trip
  • Witless Bay: O'Brien's Whale and Bird Tours — the gold standard operator
  • Elliston puffins are free — just park and walk to the headland
  • Dress for Newfoundland: waterproof layers, wind, possible cold even in June
  • Book accommodation in Twillingate very early — peak iceberg season fills completely
Pre-Trip Prep + Context
  • Read: Cod: A Biography of the Fish That Changed the World — Mark Kurlansky (excellent at 14)
  • Watch Blue Planet II Episode 2 (The Deep) for boreal ocean context
  • Watch humpback whale song recordings on YouTube before seeing them
  • Research Atlantic puffin biology — dive depth, prey, migration routes
  • Learn about the Labrador Current and its role in Iceberg Alley

Purpose — The Full Arc

JJ is 18, finished high school, and about to begin post-secondary study in marine biology or environmental science. This trip completes the arc: the North Pacific is the world's most productive marine ecosystem. It is expedition-level travel, physically demanding, scientifically rich, and emotionally significant. The graduation letter written in Newfoundland at 14 is opened and read on the final night.

The Full Arc — Age 3 to 18

Age 3: JJ watched Nemo in an aquarium and touched a stingray at SeaWorld. Age 18: JJ dives with giant Pacific octopus in cold kelp forest water off Vancouver Island. Between those two moments: tide pools, whale watches, glaciers, Bahamas reefs, Newfoundland icebergs, Hawaiian mantas, and fifteen years of a family building a child's world around curiosity, ecology, and the ocean. This trip celebrates all of that.

Itinerary

11-Day Plan

Day
1
Fly to Juneau + Mendenhall Glacier
Juneau, Alaska
Fly into Juneau. The approach over the Coast Mountains and Juneau Icefield is extraordinary — glaciers flowing in every direction. Mendenhall Glacier is 20 minutes from the airport. At age 10 JJ walked on the Athabasca Glacier. Here, the retreat markers show even faster change, and the lake below — created by meltwater — didn't exist 20 years ago.
Icefield flight approachMendenhall Glacier walkRetreat markers comparisonMendenhall Lake kayak
Learning focus: compare this glacier to Athabasca at age 10. The retreat here is measurable in years, not decades.
Day
2
Tracy Arm Fjord — Calving Glaciers
Tracy Arm Fjord
Full day boat tour into Tracy Arm — 45km of sheer granite walls, waterfalls, floating bergy bits, and the Sawyer Glacier actively calving. The calving sound — a thundercrack followed by a roar as ice the size of an apartment building hits the water — is one of the most physically arresting sounds in nature. Harbour seals haul out on ice floes. Dall's porpoises ride the bow.
Tracy Arm fjord boatSawyer Glacier calvingHarbour seals on iceGlacial geology walkDall's porpoise
Learning focus: glaciers calving into salt water add freshwater, creating a halocline that affects which species can live at different depths. Glaciers restructure ocean chemistry as they retreat.
Day
3
Humpback Bubble-Net Feeding
Chatham Strait / Icy Strait
Southeast Alaska is one of the only places where humpbacks engage in cooperative bubble-net feeding — groups of whales working together, one blowing a circular curtain of bubbles to trap herring, then all lunging upward simultaneously. Compare to the Monterey Bay whale watching at age 7 — same species, same lunge feeding, but now JJ understands the full mechanism and ecology.
Humpback bubble-net feeding tourCooperative feeding behaviourOrca sighting possibleSea otter raftBald eagle colony
Learning focus: bubble-net feeding is a learned cultural behaviour passed down through generations of Southeast Alaska humpback pods for at least 50 years. Culture exists in whale populations.
Day
4
Anan Creek — Grizzlies + Salmon + Eagles
Anan Creek Wildlife Observatory
Anan Creek is one of the most productive pink salmon streams in Southeast Alaska — both black bears and brown bears fish the falls in August. From the wildlife observatory platform, watch bears stand in the cascade and swipe. Bald eagles line the trees above. Gulls and ravens clean the carcasses. Every tree around this creek contains ocean nutrients deposited by bears carrying fish inland.
Floatplane/boat to Anan CreekBlack + brown bear salmon fishingBald eagle congregationSalmon nutrient cycleSalmon carcass observation
Learning focus: salmon are the keystone species of the North Pacific — they transfer marine nutrients to freshwater and terrestrial ecosystems. The trees around this creek are isotopically marine. Remove the salmon and the forest eventually changes.
Anan Creek requires USFS permits — book via recreation.gov months ahead. Floatplane from Wrangell or Petersburg.
Day
5
Travel to Vancouver Island
Juneau → Vancouver Island
Travel south from Alaska to Vancouver Island. Fly Juneau–Seattle–Victoria, or use the BC Ferry from Prince Rupert. Use the transit day to debrief the Alaska segment: three most important ecological ideas from the past four days. Arrive and base near Telegraph Cove for Johnstone Strait orca access.
Travel day + debriefAlaska segment reflectionBC coastal arrival
Learning focus: the ecosystems don't change at the border. The same cold North Pacific current, the same salmon species, the same orca pods cross freely. Borders are human constructs. Ecosystems are not.
Day
6
Johnstone Strait — Orca Watching
Johnstone Strait, northern Vancouver Island
Johnstone Strait has the highest density of orcas in the world in August. Morning zodiac whale watch. Afternoon kayak to sit quietly near the orca rubbing beaches (pebble beaches where orcas rub their bodies — a cultural behaviour unique to this population). You can hear them through the hull of the kayak. Southern residents are critically endangered — the conversation here is about conservation, chinook decline, vessel noise, and what it means when a culture disappears.
Southern resident orca zodiac tourOrca rubbing beach kayakPod vocalization listeningConservation discussion
Learning focus: southern resident orcas have distinct dialects — each pod communicates in a unique call repertoire passed down through matrilines. Fewer than 75 individuals remain. Their decline tracks directly to chinook salmon decline.
Best operator: Telegraph Cove Whale Watching or Stubbs Island Whale Watching. Telegraph Cove is the best base for Johnstone Strait.
Day
7
Browning Pass Kelp Forest Dive — Day 1
Browning Pass, Nigei Island area
Browning Pass is considered one of the top ten dive sites in the world and the finest cold-water Pacific dive site. Visibility can reach 30m. The kelp forest is old-growth — 20m-high bull kelp with a dense canopy, mid-water zone, and seafloor. Compare to the Monterey Bay Aquarium kelp forest exhibit at age 7 — now JJ is inside it. Wolf eels, lingcod, rockfish, nudibranchs, basket stars, spiny dogfish.
Browning Pass kelp forest diveCanopy + mid-water + seafloor transectWolf eel den observationNudibranch IDPost-dive species log
Learning focus: bull kelp grows up to 10cm per day in summer and is an annual species — the entire canopy dies back each winter and regrows. Both annual and perennial strategies can produce extraordinary ecosystems.
Dry suit essential — water is 8–12°C at depth. Book with North Island Diving or Browning Passage Dive Resort.
Day
8
Giant Pacific Octopus Dive — The Capstone
Browning Pass octopus dens
The giant Pacific octopus — up to 9m arm span, 70kg — is the largest octopus species on Earth. Browning Pass has known den sites at recreational diving depth. Hover motionless 2m from the den entrance and wait. The octopus will often emerge, assess you with direct eye contact, and change colour. This is the dive that closes the arc — from Nemo at EPCOT to GPO at Browning Pass.
GPO den approach diveColour change + texture observationBehavioural notesPost-dive journal entry
Learning focus: GPO have a lifespan of only 3–5 years yet demonstrate problem-solving, play behaviour, tool use, and individual personality. 2/3 of their neurons are in their arms. This is intelligence from a completely different evolutionary starting point.
Let the octopus decide — approach to 1.5m and wait. Your guide will know the active dens.
Day
9
Coastal Rainforest + Intertidal + Recovery
Northern Vancouver Island
Recovery day from two big dives. Walk the coastal temperate rainforest — Sitka spruce and western red cedar, salmon streams cutting through the roots, bear trails, and nitrogen-rich vegetation from marine nutrients. Find a low-tide intertidal zone: ochre sea stars, giant green anemones, purple sea urchins, chitons. Compare to Point Lobos at age 7 — better taxonomy, better ecology, better questions.
Old-growth rainforest walkSalmon stream marine nutrient tracingLow-tide intertidal ecologySea star IDComparison to age-7 Point Lobos
Learning focus: the rainforest and the ocean are one system. The nitrogen isotope signature in these trees is marine in origin — transported by salmon, processed by bears, deposited in the soil.
Day
10
Graduation Reflection Day — Open the Letter
Telegraph Cove / Victoria
The final full day. Morning beach or tide pool walk. In the afternoon: open the letter written in Newfoundland at age 14. Read it together. How much has changed? What did 14-year-old JJ not know that 18-year-old JJ now knows? What questions have been answered? What new questions replace them? Evening: graduation dinner together. This is not the end of the plan. It is the beginning of the next one.
Morning beach + tide poolOpen the Newfoundland graduation letterFull-arc reflection conversationGraduation dinner
Learning focus: childhood curiosity, consistently fed and challenged, becomes adult direction. The arc is complete. The ocean is not.
Bring the sealed letter from Newfoundland 2040. Open it together. It will be surprisingly moving.

For CJ and MJ — Closing Note

By the time this trip happens, JJ will have grown up with cattle, horses, moose, wolves, and bald eagles on the farm. He will have held a hermit crab in a California tide pool, watched a humpback breach off Newfoundland, touched a nurse shark in the Bahamas, and looked a manta ray in the eye in Hawaii. This plan works because it started with what he already knows — living animals, working land, real nature — and kept expanding that circle outward, trip by trip. The farm was always the foundation. The ocean was always the horizon. Alaska is where those two things become one.

"The farm taught him to observe. The ocean taught him why it matters."

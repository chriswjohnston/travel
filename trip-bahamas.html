Trip 05 · April 2038 · Age 12 · Grade 6 In Progress

Bahamas

Theme: Immersion Caribbean Age 12

Until now JJ has watched marine life from above — aquarium glass, boat decks, tide pool edges. The Bahamas is where that changes. He enters the water as a participant. Warm, clear Caribbean water. Coral reefs. Sea turtles. Nurse sharks. The shift from observer to field scientist.

Purpose

Move from observing marine ecosystems to entering them. Warm, clear, calm Caribbean water is the ideal first snorkeling environment. Coral reefs, tropical fish, sea turtles, and stingrays at shallow depth. This trip is about water confidence, field observation skills, and the shift from watching ocean life to being inside it.

Farm Connection

A 12-year-old who has ridden horses and worked with animals all his life already knows how to move quietly, read body language, and respect an animal's space. The reef snorkeling rules — don't touch, don't chase, observe from a respectful distance — are exactly the same protocol JJ uses with the horses. Reef animals communicate with their bodies, just like the farm animals do.

Science Questions to Build
  • What is coral — animal, plant, or mineral?
  • Why do different fish occupy different depths on a reef?
  • What is a cleaning station and why do big fish wait in line?
  • How do parrotfish eat coral and produce beach sand?
  • What does coral bleaching look like and what causes it?
  • Why are mangroves essential to reef health?
Trip Goals
  • First sustained snorkel: 30+ minutes comfortable in open water
  • Identify 10+ reef species by name using a field guide
  • Understand coral as a living animal, not a rock
  • Practice ethical wildlife approach: distance, no touch, no chase
  • Begin thinking about marine ecosystems as food webs, not just collections
Itinerary

7-Day Plan

Day
1
Arrival + Shore Snorkel Comfort Check
Nassau or Exumas
Arrive, check in, and do a 20-minute shore-entry snorkel in shallow water near the resort. No boat, no guide — just fins, mask, and snorkel. This session is purely about comfort, not observation. Is the mask sealing? Is JJ calm breathing through the tube? Build confidence before the reef days.
Gear fittingShore-entry shallow snorkelBreathing technique practice
Learning focus: comfort precedes curiosity. You can't observe anything if you're anxious about the equipment.
Day
2
First Reef Snorkel — Shallow Coral Garden
Reef site 3–6m depth
Choose a beginner reef site — coral garden, 3–6m depth, calm current. Goal: float slowly and identify coral types (brain, staghorn, elkhorn, fire coral) and common fish species (parrotfish, surgeonfish, butterflyfish, yellowtail snapper). Bring a waterproof fish ID card. After returning, name everything you saw — building the habit of observation followed by identification.
Guided shallow reef snorkelCoral type identificationFish species ID challengeField guide research
Learning focus: reefs are structured habitats — different coral architecture attracts different species.
Buy the 'Reef Fish Identification — Caribbean' waterproof card set. JJ will use it every snorkel for the rest of the trip.
Day
3
Mangrove Kayak + Beach Ecology
Mangrove system
Kayak through a mangrove system — roots that look like they're walking on water, juvenile reef fish hiding in the tangle. The connection: the same parrotfish JJ saw on the reef was born in a mangrove creek like this one. Beach walk to study the tide line: shells, coral fragments, sargassum, and what washes ashore tells you what lives offshore.
Mangrove kayak tourJuvenile fish ID in rootsBeach ecology tide-line walkShell and coral ID
Learning focus: reefs don't exist in isolation. Mangroves are nurseries, seagrass beds are feeding grounds. Cut any one link and the reef unravels.
Day
4
Nurse Sharks + Stingrays + Sea Turtles
Compass Cay / Stingray City
Compass Cay has resident nurse sharks that swim freely around the docks — harmless, docile, and curious. Stingray City in the shallows has southern stingrays swimming around and over your feet in chest-deep water. Sea turtle snorkel at a designated foraging area. Rules: no chasing, no grabbing fins, keep 2m distance, let them approach you.
Nurse shark snorkel encounterStingray City wadingSea turtle snorkelEthical wildlife approach practice
Learning focus: nurse sharks are curious, not aggressive — understanding the difference between predatory and passive behaviour is foundational marine biology.
Day
5
Deep Reef Food Web Observation
Deeper reef site 5–10m
Return to the reef with a specific assignment: map a food web. Identify producers (algae), herbivores (parrotfish, urchins), small predators (grouper, snapper), and apex predators. Look for a cleaning station — cleaner wrasses removing parasites from larger fish. Predatory grouper lined up to be cleaned by a 2-inch wrasse is one of the most striking images in marine biology.
Deep reef snorkelFood web mapping exerciseCleaning station observationCoral bleaching assessment
Learning focus: a reef is not a collection of animals — it's a functioning economy. Remove one species and cascading effects ripple through the whole system.
Day
6
JJ's Choice Day + Trip Debrief
Anywhere JJ picks
Let JJ choose the day: repeat snorkel, boat day, kayak, or beach rest. In the evening, do a full trip debrief: species list (aim for 20+), food web drawing, one thing that surprised him, and one question he wants to answer before the next trip. The question becomes a research project at home.
JJ's choiceSpecies list compilationFood web drawingPost-trip research question
Learning focus: ownership of curiosity — the transition from 'I was shown' to 'I am investigating.'

April is shoulder season for the Bahamas — good value vs peak winter rates, and weather is excellent. Only use reef-safe sunscreen — regular sunscreen (with oxybenzone) is harmful to coral and illegal in some Bahamian waters.
Gear + Logistics
  • Own mask and snorkel fitted at home before the trip — rental gear is often poor
  • Spring suit or rash guard for sun protection, not warmth
  • GoPro or phone in waterproof case — even basic underwater photos help with ID
  • Best Exumas base: Staniel Cay Yacht Club (close to Thunderball Grotto and Compass Cay)
  • Nassau is a useful transit hub — consider one night there on arrival
